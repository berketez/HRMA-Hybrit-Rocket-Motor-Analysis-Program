Hybrid rocket motors are a propulsion system that combines characteristics of both solid and liquid rocket engines. In these systems, the fuel is stored in solid form while the oxidizer is supplied in liquid or gaseous form. This configuration provides inherent safety advantages due to the physical separation of propellants and reduces the risk of catastrophic failure. Additional advantages include throttleability, shutdown capability, restart potential, and relatively low environmental impact. These features make hybrid rocket motors attractive for sounding rockets, experimental launch vehicles, and academic research platforms.

Despite their advantages, the design and optimization of hybrid rocket motors present several challenges. The most significant difficulty arises from the complex combustion process occurring at the solid fuel surface. The fuel regression rate directly controls the fuel mass flow rate and influences the oxidizer to fuel ratio throughout the burn. Accurate prediction of regression behavior is therefore essential for reliable performance estimation. This process depends on multiple interacting parameters including oxidizer mass flux, fuel composition, chamber pressure, and flow conditions.

Conventional hybrid rocket motor design approaches often rely on simplified empirical correlations or extensive experimental testing. While these methods provide useful insight, they are time consuming, costly, and limited in their ability to explore large design spaces. The increasing availability of computational tools has enabled more efficient analysis and optimization through numerical modeling and simulation.

HRMA was developed to address these challenges by providing an integrated computational environment for hybrid rocket motor analysis. The software combines propulsion performance calculations with injector design, heat transfer analysis, structural evaluation, and trajectory prediction in a modular and extensible framework. This approach allows engineers and researchers to rapidly evaluate design alternatives and gain deeper insight into hybrid rocket motor behavior. The objective of this paper is to present the structure, methodology, and capabilities of the HRMA software and to demonstrate its applicability as a reliable engineering analysis tool.
